💬 THE BIG STORY

  
 

India throws Mauritius an oil lifeline as the ceasefire wobbles

Prime Minister Navin Ramgoolam announced Thursday that Mauritius has finalised an agreement with India to guarantee the island’s petroleum and gas supply. The deal is a direct response to the energy shock unleashed by the Iran war. Mauritius imports 100% of its refined fuel, and with the Strait of Hormuz still barely open despite a two-week ceasefire, New Delhi has effectively become Port Louis’s insurance policy against a supply crunch.

The announcement came during a parliamentary session where Ramgoolam also cautioned that the two-week truce must not breed complacency, calling it a “fragile peace.” He is right to worry. Only a handful of vessels, including one tanker and several bulk carriers, have been allowed through Hormuz since the ceasefire began. Washington and Tehran are accusing each other of not honouring the agreement. Brent crude climbed back to $96.35 a barrel on Thursday, and the American benchmark WTI crossed $100 again.

For Mauritius, the India deal buys time. But with tourism minister Xavier-Luc Duval warning that the sector will be “severely impacted” sooner or later, and consumer groups demanding excise duty and VAT be scrapped on fuel, the pressure on government to act beyond diplomatic handshakes is mounting fast.

An Indian lifeline is welcome. Whether it’s enough depends on what happens in the Strait over the next twelve days.

  
 
 

🇲🇺 IN MAURITIUS

  
 

Béranger floats creating a “new MMM”

Paul Béranger is not going quietly. In a video posted on his Facebook page Thursday, the former MMM leader raised the possibility of launching an entirely new party if the current leadership proceeds with what he calls a rigged delegates’ assembly. “If they go ahead with their fake assembly and take bad decisions, then we, the true militants, will have no choice but to move forward with a new MMM,” he said.

The threat escalates what was already a crisis inside Mauritius’s oldest opposition party. Béranger resigned as leader last week and now sits as an independent with his daughter Joanna and MP Chetan Baboolall. A splinter party would divide the mauve voter base further, potentially reshaping the opposition landscape for years.

A new party or a negotiating tactic? Either way, the MMM is splitting in real time.

Air Mauritius senior manager arrested over discounted Audi deal

The Financial Crimes Commission arrested Anba Manikham, a Senior Manager at Air Mauritius, over the alleged sale of a company-owned Audi Q5 at a heavily discounted price, netting the buyer a reported Rs 550,000 benefit. Manikham and his partner Kiran Balgobin were released on bail after appearing in court. The case adds to a string of FCC investigations into public-sector dealings.

Food prices keep climbing: meat, fish, oil and cheese all up

The household shopping basket keeps getting heavier. Meat, fish, cooking oil, corned beef and cheese have all risen in price since the start of the year, driven by global supply chain disruptions and the war-related spike in shipping costs. With 99% of merchandise arriving by sea through routes now diverted around the Cape, every imported calorie carries a surcharge.

Heavy vehicles banned from five major routes during rush hour

Following a multi-vehicle collision at the Caudan roundabout, the Commissioner of Police will enforce strict restrictions on heavy goods vehicles using five arterial routes during morning peak hours. The Cabinet-noted crackdown aims to reduce the risk of accidents involving trucks on congested commuter corridors.

🔢 BY THE NUMBERS

  
 

$96.35 per barrel: Brent crude on Thursday, down from the $109 peak but still elevated as Strait of Hormuz traffic remains at a trickle. The US benchmark WTI crossed $100 again on ceasefire doubts. Mauritius imports every drop of its refined fuel. Every dollar on the barrel is a policy headache in Port Louis.

$4,757.60 per ounce: Gold surged 8.39% in a single session — its largest one-day jump in years — as investors scrambled for safety amid ceasefire uncertainty. In Mauritius, where gold is cultural wealth stored in drawers and around necks, the family jewellery box has quietly become the best-performing asset of 2026.

Rs 463 billion: Mauritius’s national reserves have slipped to this level, a sign that the twin pressures of costly fuel imports and volatile capital flows are draining the cushion faster than expected. The figure is a reminder that the India oil deal, while welcome, does not solve the underlying fiscal squeeze.

🌍 IN OUR BACKYARD

  
 

IMF warns Iran war will slow global growth

The head of the International Monetary Fund warned Thursday that the Iran conflict is darkening the outlook for the world economy — whether or not the fragile ceasefire holds. Disrupted oil flows, elevated energy prices and broken shipping routes have already baked in damage that will take quarters, not weeks, to unwind. For small island economies like Mauritius, the IMF’s warning translates into a simple forecast: imported inflation is here to stay.

Israeli strikes on Lebanon kill over 200, ceasefire at risk

Israeli airstrikes on Beirut, Tyre and southern Lebanon killed at least 203 people and wounded more than 1,000 on Wednesday. Israel says Lebanon is not covered by the US-Iran truce. Iran’s parliament speaker warned “time is running out” as the attacks threaten to collapse the broader ceasefire framework. Netanyahu has since agreed to direct talks with Lebanon in Washington next week — under heavy US pressure.

Benin votes Sunday in a one-sided presidential race

Benin heads to the polls this Sunday as outgoing President Patrice Talon steps down after a decade. Finance Minister Romuald Wadagni, 49, is the clear frontrunner, backed by the ruling bloc. His sole challenger, moderate Paul Hounkpè, faces an uphill battle in a field critics say was narrowed by design. Meanwhile, in neighbouring West Africa, a spreading Islamist insurgency is dominating Benin’s security debate.

  
 
 

🗺️ AROUND THE WORLD

  
 

Hormuz still at a trickle: both sides blame each other

Two days into the ceasefire, the Strait of Hormuz — through which a fifth of the world’s oil once flowed — remains barely open. Washington accuses Tehran of dragging its feet; Iran says the US has not met its obligations. Trump told reporters Iran is “doing a very poor job” with the waterway. Saudi Arabia has halted operations at several energy sites damaged in the conflict, further constraining global supply. The ceasefire is holding — but only just.

A ceasefire without open shipping lanes is a ceasefire on paper only.

Russia and Ukraine agree to Orthodox Easter truce

Vladimir Putin announced a ceasefire in Ukraine over the Orthodox Easter weekend, running from Saturday afternoon through Easter Sunday. France 24 confirmed the announcement. Previous holiday truces in this war have been patchy at best, but any pause in fighting offers a window for humanitarian access along the front lines.

Melania Trump denies Epstein ties in rare White House statement

US First Lady Melania Trump delivered an extraordinary statement from the White House on Thursday, denying any connection to the late financier Jeffrey Epstein and dismissing rumours that Epstein introduced her to Donald Trump as “mean-spirited attempts to defame my reputation.” She called on Congress to hold a hearing for survivors — though she did not explain why she chose this moment to speak. Former AG Pam Bondi has refused to appear before Congress on the matter.

  
 
 

🧠 THE DEEP END

  
 

Afrika Bambaataa, the man who shaped hip-hop, is dead at 68

Afrika Bambaataa, one of hip-hop’s founding pioneers, died on Thursday from prostate cancer. Born Kevin Donovan in the South Bronx, he helped forge an entire culture in the late 1970s: DJing, breakdancing, graffiti and MCing fused into what he called “hip-hop.” His 1982 track “Planet Rock,” built on Kraftwerk samples and a Roland TR-808, is one of the most influential records ever made — the template for electro, house and techno that followed.

His legacy is complicated. Bambaataa founded the Zulu Nation collective to channel gang energy into art, but longstanding abuse allegations cast a shadow over his later years. He leaves behind a genre that now generates billions and a cultural footprint felt from the Bronx to Mauritius and everywhere in between.

He turned a block party into a global movement. The world sounds different because of him.
